# Manifesto

## Why this exists

Misinformation is not primarily a technical problem. It is a cognitive one.

False claims spread because they are engineered to spread — designed to bypass the slow, effortful systems of critical reasoning and land directly in the parts of the brain that respond to fear, familiarity, and tribal belonging. A well-crafted piece of misinformation does not ask you to evaluate it. It asks you to feel it first and believe it second.

The problem compounds because the tools for resisting misinformation — logic, epistemology, source evaluation, knowledge of cognitive biases — are scattered across academic disciplines that most people never encounter. A philosopher knows about epistemic overconfidence. A statistician knows about survivorship bias. A rhetorician knows about the straw man. A media researcher knows about framing. But these fields rarely talk to each other, and almost none of that knowledge reaches the people who need it most, when they need it most: in the moment of reading a tweet, watching a reel, or forwarding a headline.

This toolkit exists to close that gap.

---

## What we believe

**That critical thinking is a skill, not a personality trait.** The ability to identify a fallacy, evaluate a source, or notice when an argument is built on an unexamined assumption is learnable. It is not reserved for academics, journalists, or unusually skeptical people. It can be taught, practiced, and eventually made second nature. But it requires tools.

**That naming things is the first step to resisting them.** You cannot effectively push back against an argument that exploits your availability heuristic if you do not know what an availability heuristic is. You cannot call out a slippery slope fallacy if you have no name for it. Precise vocabulary is not pedantry — it is the difference between a vague sense that something is wrong and the ability to articulate exactly what and why.

**That AI can be part of the solution.** Large language models have an unusual property: they can hold and apply a very large body of knowledge — about cognitive biases, logical fallacies, source evaluation criteria, scientific literacy — and apply it systematically to any input, without fatigue, without ego investment in the conclusion, and without the social pressure that makes humans hesitant to call something out. That is a genuine capability, and it deserves to be used deliberately.

**That neutrality is not the same as false balance.** Being rigorous and impartial does not mean treating all claims as equally uncertain. Some things are factually wrong. Some arguments are logically invalid. Some sources are unreliable. A fact-checking system that refuses to say so in the name of "presenting all sides" is not neutral — it is dishonest. This toolkit is designed to call things what they are.

**That the goal is not winning arguments but improving reasoning.** The purpose of identifying a fallacy in someone else's argument is not to humiliate them. It is to make the conversation more honest. The purpose of detecting a cognitive bias in a piece of content is not to feel superior to its author. It is to understand why the content is convincing, and whether it should be. The anti-fake instinct, at its best, is not combative. It is epistemic hygiene.

## What we are building against

We are building against a specific set of patterns — not against any ideology, party, or community, but against the techniques themselves wherever they appear:

- **Repetition as a substitute for evidence.** The illusory truth effect is real: hearing something many times makes it feel true regardless of whether it is. This toolkit is built to notice when repetition is doing argumentative work that evidence should be doing.

- **Emotional intensity as a substitute for logical validity.** Fear, outrage, and disgust are powerful signals. They are also routinely exploited. An argument that makes you angry is not thereby correct. This toolkit is built to separate the emotional charge of a claim from its evidentiary value.

- **Authority as a substitute for expertise.** Being well-known, credentialed, or confident is not the same as being right about this particular question. This toolkit is built to ask: expert in what, specifically, and what does the evidence actually show?

- **Complexity as a weapon.** Some misinformation works by being too simple. Some works by drowning the audience in jargon, irrelevant detail, or sheer volume of claims — the Gish Gallop — until rigorous evaluation seems impossible. This toolkit is built to cut through both.

- **The exploitation of cognitive shortcuts.** Humans are not irrational — they are boundedly rational, using heuristics that work well most of the time and fail predictably in specific conditions. Sophisticated misinformation is designed around those failure modes. This toolkit makes those failure modes visible.

---

## What this is not

This is not a tool for proving that any particular political position is correct or incorrect. It is a methodology, not a conclusion. Applied honestly, it will find problems on all sides of any debate — because cognitive biases, logical fallacies, and evidentiary failures are universal human tendencies, not the exclusive property of any group.

This is not a replacement for human judgment. AI-assisted analysis is a starting point, not a verdict. The system can identify that a claim is unverified, flag that an argument uses a false dilemma, or note that an author shows signs of confirmation bias. What you do with that information — how you weigh it, what you say, whether you share — remains your responsibility.

This is not a finished project. The reference documents will need updating. The pipelines will benefit from iteration. The examples barely scratch the surface of the domains where this system is useful. This is an open toolkit precisely because no single person or team can keep up with the full surface area of the problem.

---

## An invitation

If you find this useful, use it. If you find gaps, fill them. If you find errors, correct them and submit a pull request.

The goal is a world where more people have access to better tools for thinking clearly — and where those tools are free, open, and continuously improving.

That is a goal worth working toward.
