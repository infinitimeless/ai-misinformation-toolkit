COMPLETE LIST OF COGNITIVE BIASES BY CATEGORY

1. PERCEPTION AND ATTENTION BIASES
================================================================================

- Selective Attention Bias
  Tendency to focus on certain elements of a situation while ignoring others.
  We notice what interests us and disregard the rest.

- Change Blindness
  Inability to detect obvious changes in the visual environment when our
  attention is directed elsewhere.

- Attentional Blindness (Inattentional Blindness)
  Phenomenon where we fail to notice obvious things right in front of us
  because our attention is absorbed by something else.

- Cocktail Party Effect
  Ability to filter out auditory distractions and focus on a particular
  conversation in a noisy environment.

- Spotlighting Effect
  Tendency to focus excessively on one aspect of a situation while ignoring
  the broader context.

- Salience Bias
  Tendency to give more importance to spectacular, recent, or emotionally
  striking events.

- Serial Position Effect
  Information presented first (primacy) or last (recency) has a greater
  impact on our judgments.

- Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)                                ★ Added
  Tendency to better remember an item that stands out from its surroundings;
  distinctive stimuli are encoded more strongly in memory.

- Frequency Illusion (Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon)                       ★ Added
  After learning about something new, we start noticing it everywhere,
  creating the false impression that it has become more frequent.

2. MEMORY BIASES
================================================================================

- Reconstructive Memory Bias
  Our memory does not record facts exactly; it reconstructs them based on
  our current understanding and beliefs.

- False Memory Bias
  Involuntary creation of memories of events that never occurred or that
  unfolded differently than remembered.

- Prospective Memory Bias
  Tendency to better remember unfulfilled intentions than fulfilled ones.

- Source Forgetting Bias
  We forget the source of information but retain the information itself.

- Recency Bias
  Tendency to give more weight to recent events or information in memory.

- Primacy Bias
  First information received has a stronger impact than subsequent information.

- Implicit Memory Effect
  Past experiences influence current behavior without our conscious awareness.

- End-of-Experience Effect
  We remember the end of an experience better than its middle.

- Beginning-of-Experience Effect
  We remember the beginning of an experience better than its middle.

- Source Amnesia
  Inability to remember where we learned something while still retaining
  the information.

- Duration Neglect Bias
  Tendency to forget the actual duration of an event, generally by
  underestimating it.

- Zeigarnik Effect                                                      ★ Added
  Uncompleted or interrupted tasks are better remembered than completed ones,
  creating a cognitive tension that keeps them active in memory.

- Peak-End Rule                                                         ★ Added
  We judge past experiences almost entirely by how they felt at their most
  intense moment and at their end, rather than by the average experience.

- Telescoping Effect                                                    ★ Added
  Recent events are perceived as more distant in time, while distant events
  seem more recent than they actually are (forward telescoping).

- Google Effect (Digital Amnesia)                                       ★ Added
  Tendency to forget information that can easily be found online, as we
  rely on digital tools as external memory.

3. STATISTICAL JUDGMENT AND REASONING BIASES
================================================================================

- Availability Bias
  Tendency to overestimate the probability of events that are easily recalled
  from memory, generally because they are recent or spectacular.

- Illusion of Control
  Tendency to overestimate the degree of control we have over external events.

- Planning Fallacy
  Tendency to underestimate the time, costs, and risks of future actions.

- Confirmation Bias
  Tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information in a way that
  confirms our pre-existing beliefs.

- Coherence Bias
  Tendency to seek consistency in our beliefs and to resist contradictory
  information.

- Hindsight Bias
  Tendency to see the past as more predictable than it actually was.

- Conjunction Fallacy
  Error whereby one judges a combination of events as more probable than
  a single event alone.

- Anchoring Bias
  Tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information received
  (the anchor) when making subsequent judgments.

- Illusion of Transparency
  Overestimation of how clearly our intentions and reasons are perceived
  by others.

- Representativeness Bias
  Judgment based on resemblance to a typical example rather than on
  appropriate statistical analysis.

- False Consensus Bias
  Tendency to overestimate the degree to which others agree with our beliefs.

- False Uniqueness Bias
  Tendency to believe our flaws are more unique than our qualities.

- Optimism Bias
  Tendency to believe that negative events are less likely to happen to us
  than to others.

- Pessimism Bias
  Tendency to overestimate the probability of negative events.

- Fundamental Attribution Error
  Tendency to attribute others' behaviors to their personal dispositions
  rather than to situational factors.

- Self-Serving Bias
  Tendency to attribute our successes to internal qualities and our failures
  to external factors.

- Unrealistic Optimism Bias
  Belief that our future will be better than that of most other people.

- Deterministic Reasoning Bias
  Incorrect belief that we can predict the outcome of future random events.

- Zero Probability Bias
  Belief that an event with very low probability will never occur.

- Dead Zone Bias
  Tendency to avoid decisions in order to minimize perceived risks.

- Escalation of Commitment
  Tendency to invest more resources in a non-productive activity because
  we have already invested in it.

- Cognitive Dissonance Bias
  Tendency to reduce the mental discomfort caused by contradictory beliefs.

- Normalcy Bias
  Tendency to underestimate the potential impact of disasters and to see
  them as less probable than they actually are.

- Status Quo Bias
  Preference for the current situation even when superior alternatives
  are available.

- Illusory Correlation
  Perception of a correlation between two unrelated variables.

- Gambler's Fallacy                                                     ★ Added
  Belief that past random events influence the probability of future random
  events (e.g., believing a coin is "due" for heads after a string of tails).

- Hot-Hand Fallacy                                                      ★ Added
  Belief that a person who has experienced success in a random event has a
  greater probability of further success in subsequent attempts.

- Survivorship Bias                                                     ★ Added
  Focusing on entities that passed a selection process while overlooking
  those that did not, leading to false conclusions about success rates.

- Clustering Illusion                                                   ★ Added
  Tendency to perceive meaningful patterns or streaks in small random
  samples, even when the data is genuinely random.

- Neglect of Probability                                                ★ Added
  Tendency to completely disregard probability when making decisions under
  uncertainty, leading to irrational risk assessment.

4. SOCIAL AND INTERPERSONAL BIASES
================================================================================

- In-group Bias
  Tendency to favor and positively evaluate members of our own group while
  being critical of outsiders.

- Social Desirability Bias
  Tendency to give responses that we believe are socially acceptable.

- Self-Presentation Bias
  Tendency to manage our public image in order to impress others.

- Compliance Bias
  Tendency to agree with others in order to avoid conflict.

- Actor-Observer Bias
  Tendency to attribute our own actions to situational factors while
  attributing others' actions to their dispositions.

- Stereotype Bias
  Tendency to use simplified generalizations about a group to judge
  individuals within that group.

- Discrimination Bias
  Unfair behavior or treatment based on group membership.

- Prejudice Bias
  Preconceived opinion about a group or person, generally negative and
  based on little real experience.

- In-group / Out-group Bias
  Tendency to perceive in-group members as more similar to each other
  than they actually are.

- Halo Effect
  Tendency to let a general positive (or negative) impression influence
  the evaluation of specific traits.

- Horn Effect
  Tendency to let a single negative trait influence an overall evaluation.

- Liking Bias (Sympathy Bias)
  Tendency to evaluate positively the people we like.

- Hostile Attribution Bias
  Tendency to interpret ambiguous actions of others as hostile.

- Similarity-Attraction Bias
  Tendency to like people who resemble us more than those who differ from us.

- Cheerleader Effect                                                    ★ Added
  Tendency to perceive individuals as more attractive when seen as part
  of a group than when viewed alone.

- Cross-Race Effect                                                     ★ Added
  Greater difficulty in recognizing and distinguishing faces of individuals
  from a racial group different from one's own.

- Diffusion of Responsibility                                           ★ Added
  Reduced sense of personal obligation to act when others are present;
  the larger the group, the less any individual feels responsible.

5. BELIEF AND CONVICTION BIASES
================================================================================

- Confirmation Bias
  Tendency to search for and interpret information in a way that confirms
  our existing beliefs.

- Coherence Bias
  Tendency to maintain a coherent worldview even in the face of contradictory
  information.

- Overconfidence Bias
  Tendency to overestimate our knowledge, skills, and the accuracy of our
  predictions.

- Illusion of Expertise Bias
  Exaggerated belief in our expertise in a given field.

- Belief Reversal Bias (Backfire Effect)
  Tendency to reject evidence that contradicts our beliefs, sometimes even
  strengthening the original conviction.

- Naivety Bias (Bias Blind Spot)
  Overestimation of our own objectivity and underestimation of our biases.

- Reactance Bias
  Tendency to resist or reject attempts at influence or control.

- Illusory Truth Effect
  Belief that a statement is true simply because it has been heard many times.

- Belief-Justification Bias
  Tendency to adjust our beliefs in order to justify our past actions.

- Conviction Bias
  Tendency to maintain an initial conviction even in the face of contradictory
  evidence.

- Dunning-Kruger Effect                                                 ★ Added
  People with limited knowledge in a domain overestimate their competence,
  while genuine experts tend to underestimate their skill relative to others.

- Proportionality Bias                                                  ★ Added
  Tendency to assume that large or significant events must have equally
  large causes, making us susceptible to conspiracy-type reasoning.

- Narrative Fallacy                                                     ★ Added
  Tendency to impose coherent causal stories on events after the fact,
  creating a false sense of order and predictability in essentially random
  or complex data.

6. MOTIVATIONAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Self-Enhancement Bias
  Tendency to draw flattering conclusions about oneself in response to
  ambiguous performance.

- Self-Esteem Protection Bias
  Tendency to maintain a positive self-image by attributing successes to
  internal factors and failures to external ones.

- Illusory Superiority Bias (Lake Wobegon Effect)
  Tendency to consider oneself more competent, moral, and fair than average.

- Just-World Hypothesis
  Belief that the world is fair and that good people are rewarded while
  bad people are punished.

- Motivational Bias
  Tendency to believe and act in ways that serve our interests and desires.

- Cognitive Dissonance Reduction
  Tendency to modify our beliefs in order to reduce the mental discomfort
  caused by contradictory beliefs.

- Justification Bias
  Tendency to justify or rationalize our behaviors and beliefs.

- IKEA Effect                                                           ★ Added
  Tendency to place disproportionately high value on objects we have
  partially created ourselves, regardless of the quality of the final product.

- Pygmalion Effect                                                      ★ Added
  Higher expectations placed on a person lead to better performance,
  showing how externally motivated beliefs can shape real outcomes.

7. DECISION-MAKING BIASES
================================================================================

- Status Quo Bias
  Unreasoned preference for the current situation over alternatives,
  even superior ones.

- Framing Bias
  Decisions are influenced by the way options are presented or "framed."

- Loss Aversion Bias
  Tendency to feel the pain of a loss more intensely than the pleasure of
  an equivalent gain.

- Illusion of Control Bias
  Overestimation of the degree of control we have over events.

- Planning Fallacy
  Tendency to underestimate the time, costs, and risks of future projects.

- Sunk Cost Fallacy
  Tendency to continue investing in a project because of resources already
  committed, even when it is no longer rational to do so.

- Risk-Seeking Bias
  Tendency to take excessive risks in order to avoid a loss.

- Risk Aversion Bias
  Tendency to prefer a certain outcome over a gamble, even when the gamble
  has a higher expected value.

- Anchoring Bias
  Excessive dependence on the first piece of information received (the anchor).

- Escalation of Commitment
  Tendency to invest further in a poor decision to justify the initial
  investment.

- Outcome Bias
  Judging a decision based on its outcome rather than on the quality of
  the decision-making process.

- Default Option Bias
  Tendency to choose the default option without actively examining it.

- Hyperbolic Discounting
  Tendency to prefer immediate rewards over future rewards, even when the
  future rewards are greater.

- Dead Zone Bias
  Tendency to avoid decisions that carry any risk of loss.

- Omission vs. Commission Bias
  Tendency to perceive harm caused by action as more serious than equivalent
  harm caused by inaction.

- Choice Overload Bias (Paradox of Choice)
  Increasing difficulty in making a decision as the number of available
  options grows.

- Economic Mentality Bias
  Tendency to apply economic parameters to non-economic situations.

- Ambiguity Effect                                                      ★ Added
  Tendency to avoid options where the probability of a favorable outcome
  is unknown, preferring options with known odds even if they are worse.

- Decoy Effect                                                          ★ Added
  Introduction of a third, clearly inferior option shifts preference between
  the two original options, even though the decoy is never actually chosen.

- Ostrich Effect                                                        ★ Added
  Tendency to ignore obviously negative information or impending problems,
  delaying or avoiding decision-making to escape discomfort.

8. EMOTIONAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Mood Bias
  Our current mood influences our judgments and decisions.

- Emotional Bias
  Strong emotions disproportionately influence our beliefs and decisions.

- Emotional Salience Bias
  Information that elicits an emotional reaction is easier to memorize
  and recall.

- Projection Bias
  Tendency to project our own feelings, beliefs, and attributes onto
  other people.

- Empathy Bias
  Tendency to overestimate our ability to understand and share another
  person's feelings.

- Sentimentality Bias
  Tendency to give more weight to feelings than to facts in decision-making.

- Anxiety Bias
  When afraid, we tend to perceive situations as more threatening than they
  actually are.

- Anger Bias
  When angry, we tend to blame others and underestimate external factors.

- Sadness Bias
  When sad, we tend to be more critical and pessimistic.

- Guilt Bias
  Our feelings of guilt can influence our decisions and behaviors.

- Shame Bias
  Our feelings of shame can push us to avoid certain actions or situations.

- Love Bias
  Our feelings of love can affect our perception and judgment of a person.

- Hate Bias
  Our feelings of hate can negatively influence our perception of a person
  or group.

- Affect Heuristic                                                      ★ Added
  Judgments and decisions are guided primarily by our emotional reaction to
  a stimulus rather than by careful deliberation or analysis.

9. TEMPORAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Present Bias
  Tendency to give more importance to current events or information
  than future ones.

- Recency Bias
  Recent events have more impact on our judgments and decisions than
  older events.

- Primacy Bias
  First information received has a stronger impact than subsequent information.

- Duration Neglect (Period Neglect)
  Tendency to ignore duration when making judgments and decisions.

- Temporal Discounting Bias
  Tendency to value immediate rewards more than future ones.

- Temporal Tunnel Bias
  Tendency to ignore events that occurred outside a particular time window.

- Retrospection Bias
  Tendency to see the past as more coherent and predictable than it was.

- Chronology Bias
  Tendency to misjudge when events occurred.

- Temporal Target Bias
  Tendency to concentrate attention on a specific period of time.

10. SELF-REFERENTIAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Self-Focus Bias
  Tendency to evaluate events according to how they personally affect us.

- Asymmetric Insight Illusion
  Belief that we understand others better than they understand us.

- Spotlight Effect
  Tendency to overestimate the degree to which others notice and judge us.

- Egocentrism Bias
  Tendency to perceive and interpret the world from a self-centered perspective.

- Self-Sufficiency Bias
  Tendency to believe we are more responsible for our successes than we
  actually were.

- Actor Bias (Responsibility Bias)
  Tendency to consider ourselves responsible for outcomes even when we had
  little control.

- Endowment Effect
  Tendency to place greater value on things we own than on things we do not.

- Illusion of Control (Magical Thinking)
  Belief that our thoughts and actions can influence events that are
  independent of our control.

11. LANGUAGE AND LABELING BIASES
================================================================================

- Linguistic Bias
  The language we use influences our thoughts and perceptions.

- Naming Bias
  The way something is named influences our perception of it.

- Categorization Bias
  Our tendency to classify things into categories influences our judgments
  about those things.

- Labeling Bias
  Once a person or thing has been labeled, we tend to perceive them
  according to that label.

- Linguistic Framing Bias
  The way information is linguistically formulated influences our reaction
  to it.

- Curse of Knowledge                                                    ★ Added
  Once we know something, it becomes extremely difficult to imagine not
  knowing it, making it hard to communicate with or understand the
  perspective of those with less knowledge.

12. NORMATIVE AND CULTURAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Conformity Bias
  Tendency to conform to group norms in order to be accepted.

- Cultural Bias
  Tendency to interpret situations through the lens of our own culture
  and cultural context.

- Ethnocentrism Bias
  Tendency to perceive our own culture as superior to others.

- Tradition Bias
  Tendency to prefer what is familiar and traditional over what is new.

- Authority Bias
  Tendency to give more weight to information coming from an authority figure.

- Consensus Bias
  Tendency to evaluate an opinion as more correct if others share it.

- Social Bias
  Tendency to adopt the beliefs and behaviors of those around us.

  Tendency to adopt beliefs, behaviors, or trends because many other people

13. ADDITIONAL PERCEPTUAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Recognition Bias
  Tendency to more easily recognize things that are familiar to us.

- Perceptual Selection Bias
  We selectively perceive what matches our expectations.

- Perceptual Closure Bias
  Tendency to mentally complete incomplete shapes or images.

- Perceptual Constancy Bias
  Tendency to perceive objects as unchanged despite changes in the retinal image.

- Optical Illusion
  Inaccurate perception of sensory information, generally due to the way
  the brain processes information.

- Context Effect
  Perception of a stimulus depends on the context in which it is presented.

- Perceptual Salience Bias
  Salient or remarkable stimuli are processed faster and leave a more
  lasting impression.

- Mere Exposure Effect                                                  ★ Added
  Tendency to develop a preference for things simply because we are familiar
  with them, even without conscious recognition or direct positive experience.

14. PROCEDURAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Procedure Bias
  Tendency to evaluate outcomes based on the process used to obtain them.

- Outcome Bias
  Tendency to judge the quality of a decision based on the outcome rather
  than on the decision-making process.

- Algorithm Bias
  Tendency to overestimate the accuracy and objectivity of algorithms.

- Automation Bias
  Tendency to assume that automated processes are more objective or fair.

================================================================================

- Conjunction Error
  Incorrect judgment that a combination of events is more probable than
  a single event alone.

- Negation Error
  Difficulty correctly processing negative statements.

- Syllogistic Error
  Errors in formal logical reasoning.

- Inductive Confirmation Bias
  Tendency to seek cases that confirm our hypothesis rather than those
  that refute it.

- Hasty Generalization Bias
  Tendency to incorrectly generalize from one or two cases.

- Base Rate Fallacy
  Tendency to ignore the base frequency of an event when estimating
  its probability.

- Correspondence Bias
  Tendency to attribute behaviors to personal characteristics rather than
  to situational influences.

- Positivity Bias

- Primacy of Information Bias

17. ADDITIONAL INTERPERSONAL BIASES

- Groupthink Bias

- Illusion of Transparency

- False Uniqueness Bias

18. INFORMATIONAL AND EPISTEMOLOGICAL BIASES
================================================================================

- Source Forgetting Bias
  We forget where we learned something but remember the information itself.

- Informational Omission Bias
  Our judgments are influenced by information that is missing or not
  taken into account.

- Selective Exposure Bias
  Tendency to selectively expose ourselves to information that confirms
  our beliefs.

- First-Idea Bias
  The first idea we form about something influences our processing of
  subsequent information.

- Self-Knowledge Bias
  Tendency to consider ourselves more objective and rational than we
  actually are.

- False Knowledge Bias
  Belief that we know more than we actually do.

================================================================================
19. COMPARISON BIASES
================================================================================

- Upward Comparison Bias
  Tendency to compare ourselves to those who are better than us, which
  makes us feel dissatisfied.

- Downward Comparison Bias
  Tendency to compare ourselves to those who are worse than us, which
  makes us feel satisfied.

- Illusion of Difference Bias
  Tendency to believe we are more unique than we actually are.

- Similarity-Attraction Bias
  Tendency to like people who resemble us.

- Weber-Fechner Effect                                                  ★ Added
  Our perception of the magnitude of a change is relative, not absolute;
  the same objective difference feels smaller as the baseline grows,
  affecting judgments in pricing, effort, and sensory experience.

================================================================================

- Rationalization Bias
  Creating reasons to justify our beliefs or actions.

- Post-Hoc Justification Bias
  Creating after-the-fact explanations for our actions or decisions.

- Perspective Inversion Bias
  Inability to adopt a viewpoint different from our own.

- Internal Coherence Bias
  Tendency to maintain consistency between our beliefs and our actions.

- External Conformity Bias
  Tendency to conform to external expectations even when doing so goes

21. BOUNDED RATIONALITY BIASES
================================================================================

- Availability Heuristic
  Using ease of recall to assess probability or frequency.

- Representativeness Heuristic
  Judgment based on resemblance to a typical example.

- Affect Heuristic
  Using our emotional reaction to a stimulus to make a decision.

- Bounded Rationality
  Our limited cognitive capacities prevent us from always making
  rational decisions.

- Satisficing
  Tendency to choose the first satisfactory option rather than searching
  for the optimal one.

22. BIASES SPECIFIC TO FINANCIAL BEHAVIOR
================================================================================

- Momentum Bias
  Tendency to buy stocks that are rising and sell those that are falling,
  based on recent performance.

- Disposition Effect
  Tendency to sell winning investments too quickly and hold losing ones
  too long.

- Overconfidence Bias in Finance
  Overestimation of one's ability to predict market movements.

- Loss Aversion in Finance
  The pain of a financial loss is approximately twice as intense as the
  pleasure of an equivalent gain.

- Representativeness Bias in Finance
  Judging stocks based on recent performance rather than fundamental analysis.

- Sunk Cost Bias in Finance
  Tendency to continue investing in a poor asset because of past investments.

- Year Effect Bias
  Conviction that a company that had a good year will have another good year.

- Price Anchor Bias
  The price at which a stock previously traded influences our current
  valuation of it.
