## Module 1: Fallacies (Operational Summary)

### Formal Fallacies (Broken Logical Structure)

|Procedure|Warning Sign|
|---|---|
|**Affirming the Consequent**|"If A→B and B is true, then A is true."|
|**Circular Reasoning (Petitio Principii)**|The conclusion is already contained within the premises.|
|**Non Sequitur**|The conclusion does not logically follow from the premises.|
|**Equivocation**|A word is used in two different senses within the same argument.|

### Fallacies of Relevance (Off-Topic, Diversion)

|Procedure|Warning Sign|
|---|---|
|**Ad Hominem**|Attacks the person, not the argument.|
|**Tu Quoque / Whataboutism**|"You do the same" / "What about you?" to evade the issue.|
|**Straw Man**|Distortion of the opposing position.|
|**Red Herring**|An irrelevant element introduced to divert attention.|
|**Appeal to Emotion / Fear / Pity**|Substitutes emotion for rational argument.|
|**Appeal to Nature / Tradition**|"It is natural / we have always done it, therefore it is good."|
|**Tone Policing**|Refusal of a valid argument based on form, not substance.|

### Fallacies of Presumption (Premises Not Established)

|Procedure|Warning Sign|
|---|---|
|**False Dilemma**|"It is X or Y" — other options exist.|
|**Slippery Slope**|"X will inevitably lead to catastrophe" without justification.|
|**Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc**|"After this, therefore because of this" — correlation ≠ causation.|
|**Hasty Generalization**|General conclusion drawn from an insufficient sample.|
|**Loaded Question**|Presupposes a guilty answer.|
|**Argument from Ignorance**|"Not proven false = true."|
|**No True Scotsman**|Ad hoc redefinition to exclude counter-examples.|
|**Texas Sharpshooter**|Selecting data after the fact to simulate a pattern.|
|**False Equivalence**|Two incomparable things treated as equivalent.|
|**Motte-and-Bailey**|Extreme position defended, anodyne version invoked under pressure.|

### Fallacies of Insufficient Evidence

|Procedure|Warning Sign|
|---|---|
|**Appeal to Authority (Ad Verecundiam)**|Authority cited outside their field of expertise.|
|**Appeal to Popularity (Ad Populum)**|"Everyone thinks so, therefore it is true."|
|**Cherry Picking**|Selecting only data that confirms the thesis.|
|**Anecdote as Proof**|A specific case substituted for statistical demonstration.|
|**Survivorship Bias**|Reasoning only on visible cases.|

### Rhetorical Techniques

|Procedure|Warning Sign|
|---|---|
|**Framing**|Oriented presentation that constrains interpretation.|
|**Gish Gallop**|Avalanche of weak arguments to exhaust the opponent.|
|**Moving the Goalposts**|Changing criteria once they have been met.|
|**False Urgency**|Artificial time constraint.|
|**Obscurantism**|Drowning in jargon to intimidate.|
|**Praeterition**|Mentioning X by saying one will not mention it.|

### Interpersonal Manipulation

|Procedure|Warning Sign|
|---|---|
|**Gaslighting**|Making the other doubt their own perception.|
|**DARVO**|Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender.|
|**Projection**|Accusing the other of what one is doing oneself.|
|**Concern Trolling**|Simulated concern to undermine a position.|
|**Sealioning**|Harassment under the guise of infinite requests for proof.|

## Module 2: Cognitive Biases (Most Exploited in Disinformation)

|Bias|How Disinformation Exploits It|
|---|---|
|**Confirmation Bias**|Targeting audiences already convinced.|
|**Illusion of Truth**|Intensive repetition to make information seem "obvious."|
|**Availability Heuristic**|Spectacular events overestimated — dramatization.|
|**Anchoring Bias**|Setting a false narrative framework from the start.|
|**Salience Bias**|Catastrophism, strong emotions to mark minds.|
|**In-Group Bias**|Polarization "us vs. them."|
|**Dunning-Kruger Effect**|Exploitation of the overconfident "armchair expert."|
|**Hindsight Bias**|Rewriting history ("I told you so").|
|**Groupthink**|Group consensus suppresses critical evaluation.|
|**Post Hoc / Correlation Illusion**|Correlation presented as causation.|
|**Base Rate Neglect**|Ignoring base frequency to dramatize a statistic.|
|**Survivorship Bias**|Showing only cases favorable to the thesis.|

_For the complete catalog of biases, see `references/biais_cognitifs.md`._

## Module 3: Source Evaluation Grid

|Criterion|Questions to Ask|
|---|---|
|**Credibility**|Peer-reviewed? Official body? Expertise in this precise domain?|
|**Independence**|Conflicts of interest? Funding sources?|
|**Methodology**|How was the data produced? Is the sample sufficient?|
|**Timeliness**|Publication date? Are the data outdated?|
|**Consistency**|Confirmed by other independent sources?|
|**Context**|Was the information removed from its original context?|

## Module 4: Standard Responses by Procedure

|Detected Procedure|Response|
|---|---|
|**Cherry Picking**|"Interesting selection. However, if we look at the entirety of the data — and not just the 3% that suits the thesis..."|
|**Appeal to Authority (Out of Domain)**|"[X] is an expert in [A], which differs from [B] being discussed. A cardiologist validating economic policy is not a substitute for economic expertise."|
|**False Dilemma**|"Reducing the universe of possibilities to two options is limiting. Other options exist."|
|**Slippery Slope**|"This scenario assumes [list of implicit hypotheses] — none are justified. The slope may not be as slippery as predicted."|
|**Post Hoc**|"X occurred after Y, therefore Y caused X? By that logic, the rooster's crow causes the sun to rise."|
|**Gish Gallop**|"Impressive quantity. Quantity does not equal quality. Let us focus on the central points."|
|**Straw Man**|"What you just refuted is not what was stated. Here is what was actually advanced: ..."|
|**Ad Hominem**|"The argument stands or falls independently of who formulates it. Let us return to the facts."|

## Module 5: Author Biases (Systematic Analysis)

**Distinction:** Always distinguish between deliberate manipulation and sincere belief subject to bias. This distinction changes the diagnosis and the nature of the response.

|Frequent Bias|Signal in Text|
|---|---|
|**Confirmation Bias**|Cites only sources confirming the thesis; ignores contradictions.|
|**Hindsight Bias**|Judges past decisions with information available today.|
|**Hostile Attribution Bias**|Systematically interprets institutional actions as malicious.|
|**Overconfidence**|Concludes with certainty on data that does not support it.|
|**Representativeness Bias**|Generalizes from a single case or specific clause.|
|**Dunning-Kruger Effect**|Partial expertise (reading a contract) confused with global expertise (international pharmaceutical law).|
|**In-Group / Ideological Bias**|Adopts the narrative framework of a community (e.g., anti-institution) without questioning it.|
|**Salience Bias**|Dramatizes figures without statistical context.|

**Output Format Requirement:** Include a dedicated section **"Author Biases"** distinguishing what the author does deliberately (rhetorical procedures) from what they are likely a victim of (cognitive biases).

## Module 8: Fact-Checking Protocol

**Objective:** A systematic, neutral procedure to verify the accuracy of specific claims before accepting or refuting them.

### Step 1: Claim Isolation

- **Action:** Extract the specific factual assertion from the surrounding rhetoric.
- **Goal:** Separate the core claim (e.g., "Event X happened on Date Y") from the emotional framing or interpretation.
- **Check:** Is the claim falsifiable? (Can it be proven true or false?)

### Step 2: Primary Source Verification

- **Action:** Trace the claim back to its original source.
- **Questions:**
    - Does the source link directly to the raw data, official report, or video footage?
    - Is the source a primary document (e.g., court record, scientific paper) or a secondary interpretation (e.g., news article, blog post)?
    - Has the source been altered or taken out of context?

### Step 3: Cross-Referencing (Triangulation)

- **Action:** Seek confirmation from at least two independent, unrelated sources.
- **Criteria:**
    - **Diversity:** Sources should have different methodologies or editorial stances.
    - **Reputation:** Prioritize established fact-checking organizations, academic institutions, or official government bodies.
    - **Consensus:** Do multiple independent investigations reach the same conclusion?

### Step 4: Contextual Integrity

- **Action:** Ensure the data is presented with its full context.
- **Checks:**
    - **Timeframe:** Is the data current, or is it old data presented as new?
    - **Scope:** Is a local event presented as a global trend?
    - **Comparison:** Are statistics compared to appropriate baselines (e.g., per capita vs. absolute numbers)?

### Step 5: Methodology Review

- **Action:** Evaluate how the data was generated.
- **Questions:**
    - Was the sample size statistically significant?
    - Was the study peer-reviewed?
    - Are there conflicts of interest in the funding or authors?
    - Is the statistical method appropriate for the question asked?

### Step 6: Conclusion & Classification

- **Action:** Assign a status to the claim based on the evidence gathered.
- **Status Categories:**
    - **Verified:** Supported by multiple credible primary sources.
    - **Partially True:** Contains elements of truth but omits critical context or exaggerates scope.
    - **Misleading:** Technically accurate data used to create a false impression.
    - **False:** Contradicted by credible evidence.
    - **Unverifiable:** Insufficient evidence exists to confirm or deny.

### Common Pitfalls in Fact-Checking

- **Reverse Fact-Checking:** Starting with a desired conclusion and searching only for supporting evidence.
- **False Balance:** Giving equal weight to a fringe view and a scientific consensus when the evidence is not equal.
- **Source Contamination:** Relying on sources that themselves rely on the claim being checked (circular reporting).
- **Emotional Interference:** Allowing personal bias to influence the rigor of the verification process.
