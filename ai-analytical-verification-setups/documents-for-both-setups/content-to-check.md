From Calculator-to-Words to Swarms of Agents: My journey into the heart of the agentic revolution.

I saw a few posts about this so I decided to share my point of view.

1/20

Two years ago a friend (objectively brilliant) told me he was working on agentic frameworks. Back then there wasn’t much beyond AutoGPT and LangChain. Nothing like what we have now.

2/20

Two years earlier he was already running GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks): neural networks that compete so the best improves generation after generation. (I’m simplifying…)

I laughed. I was wrong.

3/20

Agentic systems aren’t new in computer science.

But the LLM explosion changed everything.

Here’s how we went from a chatbot to a system capable of running an entire company.

4/20

At first: a word calculator.

You send a prompt. You get an output. In natural language.

You could hack a headless browser to scrape info. But autonomy? Almost nonexistent.

5/20

Then someone asked THE question:

“From language to instructions is only one step, right?”

The idea was no longer to get answers but to get actions.

That’s where Agency was born.

6/20

We went from spectators to actors.

AI no longer tells you how to do something.

It does it. AI accomplishes digital tasks.

7/20

First frameworks. AutoGPT. BabyAGI.

Prompt chains improved. RAG emerged — an external memory usable as a tool.

LangChain organized all of that.

The ecosystem started to look like something.

8/20

Personally, I played with CrewAI, Llama.cpp, Ollama, LM Studio.

While tinkering I asked an agent to: do internet research, analyze results, synthesize a strategy.

It was impressive. But it still required terminal tinkering or getting your hands in Python.

9/20

Then reasoning models arrived.

AI no longer answered on the first pass. It thought. It looped. It refined.

A total paradigm shift.

10/20

And right after (it’s moving too fast to keep up):

We stopped saying “do this.” We say “generate an action plan with verifiable steps.”

Planning became a native AI skill.

11/20

The real challenge remained the critical bridge.

Going from natural language to computer code.

Solution? Simple in theory: GitHub is full of code, comments, docs. Train an LLM on it.

Problem solved.

12/20

Result:

You speak French to an agent. It translates to code. It “talks” to tools, apps, SaaS via their APIs.

Without you needing to know how the other end works.

That’s the real leap.

13/20

And now? The swarm.

You give a mission. The agent crafts a strategy. It creates other specialized agents. They deliver, ship and accomplish.

14/20

Imagine tomorrow:

A GitHub agent. An architect. A tester. An agent that buys the domain. One that builds the site. One that writes the business plan. One that incorporates the company. One that manages Stripe.

An entire company. Piloted by a swarm.

15/20

And that’s where my optimism meets my concern.

The barrier to starting a company collapses. Anyone with a good idea and a good prompt can launch a startup.

But…

16/20

Markets will be saturated with automatically generated products.

Human quality and intent will become the only real value criteria.

The developer no longer types every line. They design the architecture, supervise the swarm, validate results.

17/20

Dev cycles from 6 months → a few days.

But who is responsible if an agent makes a catastrophic financial decision?

Our laws are made for humans. Not for swarms of algorithms.

18/20

The real risk isn’t technical. It’s societal.

AI helps improve AI, the agents, their autonomy, the hardware — the whole ecosystem that in turn improves AI.

A virtuous… or vicious feedback loop, depending on where you sit in the chain.

19/20

What are the chances a business leader prefers to deal with humans when they can have agent teams available 24/7 for 10% of the cost?

No payroll taxes. No works council. No unions.

What kind of CEO refuses that?

20/20

We’re moving from the era of content generation to the era of action generation.

The question is no longer “will this happen?” It’s “are we ready?”

Honestly? Not yet. But that’s exactly why we need to talk about it now.
