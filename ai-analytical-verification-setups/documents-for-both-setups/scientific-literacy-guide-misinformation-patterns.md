## 1. Correlation vs Causation

- Correlation: two variables move together
- Causation: one variable directly affects another

Correlation alone does NOT prove causation.

---

## 2. Types of Studies

### Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)
- Participants randomly assigned
- Strongest evidence for causation

### Observational Studies
- No intervention
- Can show correlation, not definitive causation

### Meta-Analysis
- Combines multiple studies
- High reliability if well conducted

---

## 3. Statistical Concepts

### Sample Size
- Larger samples = more reliable results

### Statistical Significance
- Indicates likelihood result is not due to chance
- Does NOT measure importance

### Effect Size
- Measures magnitude of effect
- More meaningful than significance alone

---

## 4. Common Misinterpretations

- Small studies treated as definitive
- Relative risk exaggerated without absolute risk
- Ignoring confidence intervals
- Overstating weak evidence

---

## 5. Scientific Consensus

- Built over time through multiple studies
- More reliable than single studies
- Should be prioritized in analysis

---

## 6. Warning Signs

- Extraordinary claims without strong evidence
- Overreliance on one study
- Misuse of technical terms
- Lack of peer review
