

## Purpose
Assess the reliability and credibility of information sources.

---

## 1. Core Criteria

### Authority
- Who is the author?
- What are their qualifications?
- Are they recognized in the field?

### Accuracy
- Are claims supported by evidence?
- Are sources cited?
- Can information be verified elsewhere?

### Transparency
- Is methodology explained?
- Are limitations acknowledged?
- Are conflicts of interest disclosed?

### Independence
- Is the source free from political/commercial bias?
- Who funds or owns the source?

### Currency
- Is the information up to date?
- Is timing relevant to the claim?

---

## 2. Source Types (General Reliability)

High reliability:
- Peer-reviewed journals
- Official institutions
- Government databases
- Scientific organizations

Moderate reliability:
- Established media outlets
- Expert interviews

Low reliability:
- Blogs without sources
- Anonymous content
- Social media posts without evidence

---

## 3. Red Flags

- No sources cited
- Emotional or sensational language
- Absolute claims ("always", "never")
- Selective data use
- Misleading headlines

---

## 4. Cross-Verification

Always check:
- Multiple independent sources
- Consistency across sources
- Agreement with established knowledge

---

## 5. Conclusion

A source should never be trusted in isolation.
Reliability increases with:
- consistency
- transparency
- corroboration
