# Procedures to Distort a Debate or Manipulate Your Interlocutor

---

## I. Formal Fallacies

_(errors in the logical structure of reasoning)_

|Procedure|Description|
|---|---|
|**Affirming the Consequent**|_If P then Q, Q is true, therefore P is true_ — invalid|
|**Denying the Antecedent**|_If P then Q, P is false, therefore Q is false_ — invalid|
|**Invalid Syllogism**|Incorrect syllogistic structure|
|**Circular Reasoning** _(petitio principii)_|The conclusion is already contained in the premises|
|**Non Sequitur**|The conclusion does not logically follow from the premises|
|**Equivocation**|Exploiting the double meaning of a word within the same argument|
|**Amphibology**|Exploiting the syntactic ambiguity of a sentence|
|**Composition / Division**|What is true of a part is true of the whole, or vice versa|

---

## II. Informal Fallacies — Relevance

_(the argument is off-topic or diverts attention)_

|Procedure|Description|
|---|---|
|**Ad Hominem**|Attacking the person rather than the argument|
|**Circumstantial Ad Hominem**|Disqualifying the argument based on the alleged interests of the author (_"you say that because…"_)|
|**Tu Quoque**|_"You do the same thing"_ — disqualifying a criticism by the critic's behavior|
|**Whataboutism**|Deflecting to another issue to evade (_"and you, what have you done?"_) — a variant of _tu quoque_|
|**Straw Man**|Distorting the opposing position to make it easier to attack|
|**Red Herring**|Introducing an unrelated element to divert attention|
|**Appeal to Emotion**|Substituting an emotional appeal for a rational argument|
|**Appeal to Fear**|Provoking fear to make a proposal accepted|
|**Appeal to Pity** _(ad misericordiam)_|Soliciting compassion to win the argument|
|**Appeal to Flattery**|Complimenting the interlocutor to obtain their adherence|
|**Appeal to Nature**|_"It's natural, therefore it's good"_|
|**Appeal to Tradition**|_"We've always done it this way"_|
|**Appeal to Novelty**|_"It's new, therefore it's better"_|
|**Appeal to Consequences**|Denying a truth because its consequences are unpleasant|
|**Moralizing**|Transforming a factual argument into a moral judgment to disqualify it|
|**Tone Policing**|Refusing a valid argument by criticizing the manner in which it is expressed|

---

## III. Informal Fallacies — Presumption

_(the argument rests on unestablished premises)_

|Procedure|Description|
|---|---|
|**False Dilemma** _(false dichotomy)_|Presenting two options as the only possible ones when others exist|
|**Slippery Slope**|Asserting that a small action will inevitably lead to extreme consequences, without justification|
|**Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc**|_"After this, therefore because of this"_ — confusion between correlation and causation|
|**False Cause**|Identifying an erroneous causality|
|**Hasty Generalization**|Drawing a general conclusion from an insufficient sample|
|**Accident / Rigid Generalization**|Applying a general rule to a particular case that should be exempt|
|**Loaded Question** _(complex question)_|Asking a question that presupposes a guilty answer (_"Have you stopped lying?"_)|
|**Argument from Silence**|Concluding that something is true because it has not been contradicted|
|**Argument from Personal Incredulity**|_"I can't conceive how X could be true, therefore X is false"_|
|**Special Pleading**|Claiming an exemption from a rule without justifying it|
|**No True Scotsman**|Redefining a category ad hoc to exclude inconvenient counter-examples|
|**Texas Sharpshooter**|Selecting data after the fact to simulate a pattern|
|**Nirvana Fallacy**|Rejecting an imperfect solution in favor of an unrealistic ideal solution|
|**Shifting the Burden of Proof**|Imposing on the adversary the burden to prove a negative assertion|
|**False Equivalence**|Presenting two things as comparable when they are not|
|**False Balance**|Giving equal weight to two positions of vastly unequal weight|
|**Motte-and-Bailey**|Defending an extreme position (_bailey_), then retreating to an innocuous version (_motte_) when attacked|

---

## IV. Informal Fallacies — Insufficient Evidence

|Procedure|Description|
|---|---|
|**Appeal to Authority** _(ad verecundiam)_|Citing an authority outside their area of expertise|
|**Appeal to the People** _(ad populum)_|_"Everyone thinks so, therefore it's true"_|
|**Appeal to Ignorance** _(ad ignorantiam)_|_"We can't prove the opposite, therefore it's true"_|
|**Cherry Picking**|Selecting only data that confirms one's thesis|
|**Anecdote as Proof**|Substituting a particular case for a statistical demonstration|
|**Survivorship Bias**|Reasoning only on visible cases, ignoring unobserved cases|
|**Gambler's Fallacy**|Believing that past events influence independent probabilities|
|**Fallacy Fallacy**|_"This argument is a fallacy, therefore its conclusion is false"_ — invalid|

## V. Rhetorical Persuasion Techniques

_(neutral in themselves, but exploitable for deception)_

|Procedure|Description|
|---|---|
|**Framing**|Presenting information from an oriented angle that constrains interpretation|
|**Anchoring**|Setting a first piece of information that biases all subsequent judgments|
|**Loaded Language**|Using terms with strong connotations to induce emotion|
|**Euphemism / Dysphemism**|Softening or hardening reality through word choice|
|**Dog Whistle**|Sending a coded message to a specific group, imperceptible to others|
|**Thought-Terminating Cliché**|A ready-made formula that blocks reflection (_"that's life"_, _"we can't do anything"_)|
|**Preterition**|Saying one will not mention X while mentioning it|
|**Insinuation**|Suggesting without asserting, to avoid being held accountable|
|**Fear Rhetoric**|Constructing a catastrophic scenario to create urgency|
|**Appeal to Complexity** _(obscurationism)_|Drowning in jargon or technicality to intimidate|
|**Information Overload** _(Gish Gallop)_|Multiplying low-quality arguments in quantity to exhaust the adversary|
|**False Concession**|Seeming to admit a point to better counter-attack|
|**Moving the Goalposts**|Changing the criteria of a demonstration once they have been met|
|**Semantic Reframing**|Redefining a term during a debate to change the stakes|

---

## VI. Interpersonal Manipulation Techniques

_(act on the psychology of the interlocutor)_

|Procedure|Description|
|---|---|
|**Gaslighting**|Making someone doubt their own perception of reality|
|**DARVO**|_Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender_ — deny, attack, reverse roles|
|**Projection**|Accusing the other of what one does oneself|
|**Triangulation**|Introducing a third party to create insecurity or jealousy|
|**Love Bombing**|Excess affection to create emotional dependency|
|**Isolation**|Gradually cutting the interlocutor off from other sources of information|
|**Minimization**|Reducing the importance of a fault or pain (_"you're exaggerating"_)|
|**Guilt-Tripping**|Generating a sense of guilt to obtain submission|
|**Creating Doubt**|Multiplying uncertainties to paralyze adverse judgment|
|**Concern Trolling**|Simulating benevolent concern to undermine a position|
|**Sealioning**|Harassing under the guise of politely formulated endless requests for proof|
|**Sea of Ambiguity**|Remaining deliberately vague to be able to deny or reinterpret|
|**False Urgency**|Imposing an artificial time constraint to short-circuit reflection|
|**Managing the Overton Window**|Progressively shifting the limits of the sayable to render acceptable what was not|
